Flow Transect The next step for determining the maximum depth and velocity is to establish a flow transect from the shoreline, through the building site, to the inundation limit. The basic approach is to establish a point on shore at which a line parallel to the shore, averaged over 304 m (1,000 ft) [i.e., ±152 m (±500 ft) on either side], has a normal line that passes through the building site. Consider building site A in Fig. 5. The flow transect line illustrates an appropriate transect for this site (the same transect could be used for any building site that falls on the line). This transect then needs to be rotated ±22.5º about the building site (site A), to obtain a “cone’ that extends from the building site to the inundation line. The design flow conditions may be obtained from the transect within this cone, which results in the most conservative conditions. As explained in the commentary of ASCE/SEI 7-16, the requirement for considering this 45° arc reflects some variability in the flow direction, and hence the direction of force, and a desire to obtain conservative design values. There can be several challenges to determining the appropriate transect for certain practical conditions. The most easily identifiable transect normal to the shore going through Site B intersects the inundation line before reaching the site, and a different transect must be chosen. Site C has multiple potential transects. One possibility is to use the closest coast normal transect; however, engineering judgment would need to be used by applying an understanding of the likely direction of flow. The standard also allows a site-specific analysis to be used. If one is carried out, then it can be used to determine the direction of flow, and an appropriate transect can be chosen. Once the flow transect has been determined, the distance from the shore to the inundation limit, xR, can be determined. Also, the ground elevations along the transect with a maximum horizontal spacing of 30.5 m (100 ft) must be determined from the inundation limit to the building site (or possibly the shore). The result is a set of points (x, z), with the x-coordinate increasing in the direction from shore to the inundation limit (i.e., directed inland), and z is directed upward. For subsequent computation, it is convenient if the first point is at the inundation limit and the last point is at the building site. Also, along the transect, appropriate values for Manning’s coefficient must be chosen. The standard provides common values for different surfaces that range from open land to urban development. Other values from the literature can be used [see, e.g., Bunya et al. (2010)]. It should be noted that it is fairly common for the run-up modeling to be based on a uniform value of 0.03, which is close to that of bare earth. However, the commentary states that the inherent conservatism designed into the EGL analysis typically would not require the use of a value > 0.05_CD ._SENT
